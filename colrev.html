About the Colonial Revival Movement

The Colonial Heritage Farm has been particularly inspired by the historic preservation efforts at the National Colonial Farm in Accokeek, MD, the Claude Moore Colonial Farm in McLean, VA and the Great Hopes Plantation at Colonial Williamsburg.  Additionally, the Farm’s ongoing preservation efforts are a product of the “Colonial Revival Movement.”

The Colonial Revival Movement emerged in 1876 with America's Centennial celebration.  Historic preservation formed the core of the Colonial Revival Movement.  As a result, there was a growing interest in preservation of historic properties.  Private individuals and preservation societies purchased historic plantation homes and restored and furnished them for personal and public use.  Colonial Williamsburg is one such example, among many, of this interest in historical preservation inspired by the Colonial Revival movement.

With the Colonial Revival Movement, interest in American colonial history flourished. Artists, writers, architects and antique collectors helped to bring such idealized images of early American colonial life to the wider public.

The early American colonial era was looked upon as a golden age of American values and traditions, when patriots heroically fought for our independence in conjunction with America's Founding Fathers.

But at its heart, the Colonial Revival Movement romanticized the American Revolution and the “Spirit of '76.” Nothing captured this better than the “Spirit of '76” painting (previously known as Yankee Doodle) by Archibald Willard.

The “Spirit of '76” painting depicts the patriotic spirit surrounding the American Revolution and the attitude of self-determination and individual liberty made manifest in the Declaration of Independence. The painting portrays three soldiers of the American Revolution. Though one is wounded, the soldiers march on with spirit and determination.

Indeed, the Colonial Heritage Farm endeavors to keep alive some of this “Spirit of '76.” In fact, the Farm's logo incorporates Willard's “Spirit of '76” painting.
